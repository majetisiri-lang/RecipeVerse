Design philosophy: RecipeVerse is "Instagram for recipes" — but simpler. Every screen should feel like it belongs in the kitchen. No clutter, no dark patterns, no endless scroll traps. Just food, community, and creativity.

01 — Onboarding & sign up
The very first thing a new user sees. Must communicate RecipeVerse's value in 3 seconds and make signing up feel effortless. First impressions are everything.
/welcome   /signup   /login
🌐 Public First impression
What's on screen
  • RecipeVerse logo + tagline ("Share your culinary magic")
  • 3 swipeable intro slides with food photography
  • Large "Continue with Google" button (primary CTA)
  • "Sign up with email" secondary option
  • "Already have an account? Log in" link
  • Cuisine interest picker (6–8 options with food photos)
  • 3 suggested creators to follow immediately
  • Progress dots showing onboarding steps
User actions
  • swipe Through intro slides
  • tap "Continue with Google" — fastest path
  • type Email + password for email sign up
  • tap Cuisine interests (multi-select)
  • tap Follow suggested creators
  • tap "Skip" to go straight to feed
  • tap Log in if returning user
UX notes
  • Google sign-up must be the biggest, most prominent button. Most users will use it.
  • Keep the onboarding to max 3 steps — welcome, pick cuisines, follow creators.
  • The "Skip" option must always be visible. Never trap users.
  • After onboarding, the feed must never be empty — show trending if no follows yet.
  • Show real food photos during onboarding — not illustrations. Makes it feel alive.
💡
The goal of onboarding is to get the user to their first "wow moment" as fast as possible. That moment is seeing a recipe so good they immediately save it or follow the creator. Everything in onboarding should serve that goal.

02 — Home feed
The main daily-use screen. Shows recipes from followed creators. This is the habit loop — users open the app, see new recipes, engage, repeat. Must feel alive and personal.
/feed
📍 Tab 1 🔐 Auth required Daily driver
What's on screen
  • Top bar: RecipeVerse logo + search icon + notification bell with badge
  • Horizontal cuisine filter pills (All, Italian, Asian, etc.)
  • Stories-style row of creator avatars at top (new posts)
  • Recipe cards in vertical scroll — full-width with large food photo
  • Each card: photo, title, creator avatar + name, cook time, like + save buttons
  • Star rating preview on card (e.g. ★ 4.8)
  • Floating "+" create button (bottom right, always visible)
  • Pull-to-refresh gesture supported
User actions
  • tap Recipe card → opens recipe detail
  • tap Heart icon → like instantly
  • tap Bookmark icon → save to collection
  • tap Creator avatar → opens their profile
  • tap Cuisine pill → filters the feed
  • tap Bell → opens notifications
  • scroll Down → loads more recipes
  • pull Down → refreshes feed
  • tap "+" button → opens create screen
  • tap Creator story ring → opens their latest recipe
UX notes
  • Recipe photo should be 16:9 or square — tall enough to look great, short enough to see 1.5 cards at once (teases more content below).
  • Like and save buttons must respond instantly (optimistic UI — update before server confirms).
  • Empty feed state: show trending recipes with message "Follow more creators to personalise your feed."
  • The floating "+" button should always be visible. Creating content is the most important action on the platform.
  • Show cook time on the card — it's the #1 thing users look for before tapping.
💡
The stories row at the top (like Instagram) is a powerful engagement tool — it shows which creators you follow posted recently and creates FOMO. It's a low-effort high-reward feature to add in Week 5 of the build.

03 — Explore / discover
Discovery for everyone — especially new users who don't follow anyone yet. Shows trending recipes, top creators, and browseable categories. This is how RecipeVerse grows virally.
/explore
📍 Tab 2 🌐 Public Discovery engine
What's on screen
  • Large search bar at top (auto-focused when tapped)
  • Section: "Trending today" — 5 recipe cards horizontal scroll
  • Section: "Browse by cuisine" — grid of cuisine cards with food photos
  • Section: "Top creators this week" — horizontal scroll of creator chips
  • Section: "New this week" — latest published recipes
  • Section: "Quick meals (under 30 min)" — curated collection
  • Infinite scroll of all public recipes below sections
User actions
  • tap Search bar → opens search screen
  • tap Recipe card → opens detail
  • scroll Horizontal within sections
  • tap Cuisine card → filtered results
  • tap Creator chip → their profile
  • tap Like / save directly from card
  • scroll Down → more recipes
UX notes
  • Use a 2-column recipe grid (not single column) — shows more content and feels more like Pinterest/Instagram explore.
  • Trending algorithm: weight by (likes + saves) in last 24 hours. Simple and effective.
  • Cuisine cards should use beautiful food photography, not icons. Visual = emotional.
  • Never show the same recipe twice in one session.
  • The explore page is SEO gold — it should be server-side rendered for Google indexing.
💡
Explore is where RecipeVerse grows. When a non-user sees a shared recipe link, they land on this page after viewing it. Make it instantly impressive — the best recipes should be front and centre, not hidden behind a scroll.

04 — Recipe detail
The most important screen in the app. Where users actually read and cook a recipe. Must be beautiful, easy to follow in the kitchen, and encourage engagement. This screen is also what gets shared and drives viral growth.
/recipe/:id
🌐 Public Most important screen Most shared
What's on screen
  • Full-width hero food photo (swipeable if multiple)
  • Recipe title in large bold text
  • Creator row: avatar, name, follow button, verified badge
  • Quick stats bar: cook time · servings · difficulty · avg rating
  • Like, save, share action buttons (sticky bar)
  • Description / story paragraph
  • Cuisine + tag chips
  • Ingredients section with checkboxes (tap to cross off)
  • Step-by-step instructions with large step numbers
  • Optional photo per step
  • Notes section at the bottom
  • Star rating widget (tap to rate)
  • Reviews section (top 3 shown, "see all" link)
  • Comments section with reply threads
  • "More from [creator]" horizontal scroll
User actions
  • tap Heart → like recipe
  • tap Bookmark → save to collection
  • tap Share → copy link or share sheet
  • tap Follow → follow creator
  • tap Ingredient checkbox → cross off while cooking
  • tap Step number → mark step done
  • tap Star rating → leave a review
  • type Comment → post to thread
  • tap Reply on comment
  • tap Creator avatar → their profile
  • tap Related recipe → opens it
  • tap Tag chip → filtered explore
UX notes
  • Sticky action bar (like, save, share) should stay at the bottom as you scroll. Never lose it.
  • Ingredient checkboxes are a kitchen UX killer feature — users tick off as they add each ingredient. Simple, no extra feature needed.
  • Step numbers should be very large (40px+). When your hands are covered in flour, you need to tap the right step with your elbow.
  • "Keep screen on" while on this page — nothing worse than your phone locking while you're mid-cook.
  • Share button should generate a beautiful Open Graph preview with the food photo for social sharing.
  • SEO: recipe title in H1, schema.org/Recipe markup, fast load under 2s.
💡
Every shared recipe link is free marketing. When someone shares this page to WhatsApp, their friends see the food photo and recipe title in the preview. Design this page to be the most beautiful food page on the internet — that's your viral loop.

05 — Create recipe (hub screen)
The entry point for all content creation. Users land here and choose how they want to create: type manually, record by voice, or import from social. This choice screen sets the tone for the whole creation experience.
/create
📍 Tab 3 (centre) 🔐 Auth required Signature feature
What's on screen
  • Header: "Share a recipe"
  • 3 large creation mode cards (equal size, visually appealing)
  • Mode 1: 🎙️ Voice — "Speak your recipe" (highlighted as recommended)
  • Mode 2: ✍️ Type — "Write it yourself"
  • Mode 3: 🔗 Import — "Paste a link"
  • Each card has an icon, title, and 1-line description
  • Drafts section below: "Continue where you left off" (if any drafts exist)
User actions
  • tap Voice card → opens voice recorder
  • tap Type card → opens recipe form
  • tap Import card → opens URL paste screen
  • tap Draft → continues editing it
  • swipe Down → dismisses (modal style)
UX notes
  • Voice should be visually prominent — it's the differentiator. Give it a slightly larger card or an "AI-powered" badge.
  • This screen should feel exciting, not like filling out a form. Use warm colours, big icons.
  • Show a "Tip" each time: "Did you know? You can import directly from Instagram!"
  • The create button in the nav tab bar should be a big orange circle (+), not a small icon. Make it unmissable.
💡
The create screen is where creators are born. Make it feel like opening a blank canvas — full of potential. If it feels like a form, creators will post less. If it feels like a creative tool, they'll post more.

06 — Voice recording screen
RecipeVerse's most unique feature. The creator speaks their recipe naturally, the AI structures it. The UX here must feel magical — minimal, focused, and reassuring. No distractions while recording.
/create/voice
🔐 Auth required Signature AI feature
What's on screen
  • Full-screen dark background (focus mode)
  • Large animated microphone icon in centre
  • Real-time audio waveform visualiser
  • Recording timer (00:00 counting up)
  • Hint text: "Speak your recipe naturally — title, ingredients, steps"
  • Example prompt: "e.g. 'Spicy pasta for 4 people, you'll need 200g pasta...'"
  • Stop button (large, red)
  • Cancel button (top left)
  • After stop: AI processing animation with "Structuring your recipe..." message
  • Then transitions to: AI Review Screen (all fields pre-filled)
User actions
  • tap Mic button → starts recording
  • tap Stop → ends recording + triggers AI
  • tap Cancel → goes back to create hub
  • tap Re-record → if unhappy with first attempt
  • Wait → AI processes (5–10 seconds)
  • Auto-advance to review screen when done
UX notes
  • The waveform visualiser is critical — it shows the user their voice is being captured. Without visual feedback, people feel like it's not working.
  • Keep the screen dark and distraction-free during recording. This is focus time.
  • Show a gentle hint about what to say before the user starts — many people won't know how to begin speaking a recipe.
  • The AI processing screen should show a spinner + warm message, not a blank screen. "Structuring your recipe..." feels like magic.
  • If AI processing fails, show a friendly error with "Try again" and "Type it instead" options.
💡
The moment between tapping "Stop" and seeing the AI-filled review screen is the magic moment of RecipeVerse. Users should think "wait, it actually worked?!" Design this transition to feel special — a satisfying animation, a warm message, then a perfectly structured recipe appears. This is your product's wow moment.

07 — Social import screen
Creator pastes a URL from Instagram, YouTube, or Facebook and AI extracts the recipe. Lowers the barrier for creators who already have content elsewhere and want to repost it on RecipeVerse.
/create/import
🔐 Auth required Onboarding accelerator
What's on screen
  • Header: "Import from social"
  • Supported platform icons: Instagram · YouTube · Facebook
  • Large URL input field with placeholder "Paste your link here"
  • Paste from clipboard button (auto-detect if link is already copied)
  • "Import" button (disabled until URL is valid)
  • Processing state: spinning animation + "Fetching recipe content..."
  • Rights confirmation checkbox: "I own or have rights to this content"
  • Tips: "Works best with recipe videos that have captions or descriptions"
User actions
  • type or paste URL into field
  • tap "Paste from clipboard" shortcut
  • tap Import → triggers AI fetch
  • tap Rights checkbox (required)
  • Wait → AI processes and fills fields
  • Auto-advance to review screen
  • tap Cancel → back to create hub
UX notes
  • Auto-detect if the user has a URL copied to clipboard and pre-fill the input. This removes friction enormously.
  • Show platform logos prominently — not just text. Creators immediately recognise Instagram's gradient and YouTube red.
  • If the URL fails (private post, no caption), show a friendly message: "We couldn't extract the recipe. Try typing it instead." Don't leave users stuck.
  • The rights checkbox is both legal protection and an education moment. Keep it simple — one line, not a legal wall of text.
💡
For a food creator with 50 Instagram posts, this screen means they can have 50 recipes on RecipeVerse in an afternoon. That's a massive content seeding opportunity. Make this screen feel fast and smooth — speed is the entire value prop here.

08 — Creator profile (other user's)
The public page of any creator. Where followers come to browse all their recipes and new visitors decide whether to follow. Must communicate a creator's identity and make their best recipes front and centre.
/profile/:username
🌐 Public Discovery page
What's on screen
  • Cover banner (colourful gradient if no photo set)
  • Large avatar photo overlapping banner
  • Display name + @username + verified badge (if applicable)
  • Bio text (up to 160 chars)
  • Location + website link (if set)
  • Stats row: X Recipes · X Followers · X Following
  • Large Follow / Following button (primary CTA)
  • Pinned recipe (creator can pin 1 recipe to top)
  • Recipe grid: 3 columns, square thumbnails
  • Filter tabs: "Recipes" / "Saved" (own profile only)
User actions
  • tap Follow → follow this creator
  • tap Following → shows unfollow option
  • tap Recipe thumbnail → opens recipe detail
  • tap Followers count → see follower list
  • tap Following count → see following list
  • tap Website link → opens browser
  • scroll Down → loads more recipes
  • tap Share → share profile link
UX notes
  • The Follow button must be the most prominent element after the avatar. It's the primary conversion action on this page.
  • Show recipe count prominently — "24 Recipes" tells a new visitor this creator is prolific and worth following.
  • Recipe grid: 3 columns of square thumbnails is Instagram-standard. Users intuitively know this pattern.
  • The pinned recipe is a powerful creator tool — they pin their best work to always be first. Give creators this control.
  • Load the above-fold content (avatar, name, bio, follow button) instantly before the recipe grid loads.
💡
The creator profile is their storefront. Design it to make even a creator with 5 recipes look impressive. Good use of whitespace, beautiful food thumbnails, and a clean bio layout can make a new creator feel proud to share their profile link.

📍 Tab 4 🔐 Auth required Retention anchor
What's on screen
  • Header: "My Recipe Box" + total saved count
  • "All Saved" default collection (always first)
  • User-created collections shown as large cards with food photo collage
  • "+ New Collection" button
  • Each collection card: name, recipe count, 4-photo collage thumbnail

  • tap Collection → opens it to show recipes
  • tap "+ New Collection" → name it and create
  • tap Recipe in collection → opens detail
  • hold Recipe → move to different collection
  • tap Unsave icon → removes from collection
  • hold Collection → rename or delete
  • tap Sort → changes order
UX notes
  • Collections should feel like Pinterest boards — visual, personal, browseable. Use the food photos to make each collection feel unique.
  • The 4-photo collage as collection thumbnail is a small design detail that makes collections feel alive and content-rich.
  • Empty state: "You haven't saved any recipes yet. Find something delicious →" with a link to Explore. Never show a blank sad screen.
  • Collection creation should be instant — a simple text popup to enter a name, then done. No multi-step flow.

11 — Search results
Intent-driven screen. Users come here knowing what they want. Must surface results instantly and make filtering effortless. Bad search = users leave the platform to find the recipe on Google.
/search?q=...
🌐 Public Intent-driven
What's on screen
  • Search bar at top (always in focus)
  • Recent searches (before typing)
  • Trending search terms (before typing)
  • After typing: instant results appear (debounced, 300ms)
  • Filter bar: All · Cuisine · Dietary · Time · Rating
  • Sort: Relevant / Newest / Top rated / Most saved
  • Result tabs: Recipes · Creators
  • Recipe results: 2-column grid with photo, title, rating, time
  • Creator results: list with avatar, name, recipe count, follow button
  • Result count: "47 recipes for 'chicken'"
User actions
  • type Search term → instant results
  • tap Filter pill → opens filter drawer
  • tap Sort → changes result order
  • tap Recipes/Creators tab → switches view
  • tap Recipe result → opens detail
  • tap Creator result → opens profile
  • tap Follow (in creator result) → follows
  • tap Recent search → re-runs it
  • tap × on search → clears and shows defaults
UX notes
  • Results should appear as the user types — not after pressing enter. 300ms debounce feels instant.
  • If no results: show "No recipes for X. Try these similar searches:" — never a dead end.
  • Filters should be a bottom drawer (slide up from bottom), not a full new screen. Feels faster and less disruptive.
  • Save the last 5 searches. Users frequently repeat searches ("I know I searched for pasta last week").
  • Search by ingredient is the power feature — searching "chicken" returns all recipes where chicken is an ingredient, not just in the title.

The engagement loop closer. This screen brings users back into the app after they've left. Good notifications feel personal and exciting. Bad ones feel spammy and get turned off.
/notifications
🔐 Auth required Retention tool
What's on screen
  • Header: "Notifications" + unread count
  • "Mark all as read" button
  • Notification list, newest first
  • Each notification: actor avatar, short message, time, thumbnail (if recipe)
  • Unread notifications have orange left border or light background
  • Notification types: followed you · liked your recipe · commented · replied · new recipe from [creator]
  • Empty state: "Nothing here yet — start following creators!"

  • tap Notification → navigates to relevant content
  • tap Actor avatar → opens their profile
  • tap Mark all read → clears badge
  • swipe Left on notification → dismiss it
  • scroll Down → loads older notifications

  • Keep notification text short and human: "Ji-Yeon saved your Jollof Rice 🔖" not "User [id] performed save action on recipe [id]".

13 — Settings
Account and preference management. Should be simple and honest — no dark patterns, no hidden options to delete your data. Users should feel in control.
/settings
🔐 Auth required
Settings groups
  • Account: Edit profile, change email, change password
  • Notifications: Toggle each type on/off (new follower, likes, comments, new recipes)
  • Privacy: Public/private account, blocked users
  • Content: Dietary preferences for feed filtering
  • About: Terms, Privacy Policy, version number
  • Danger zone: Delete account (requires confirmation)
  • Log out button (always prominent)
User actions
  • tap Edit profile → opens edit form
  • tap Notification toggles → enable/disable
  • tap Privacy toggle → public/private
  • tap Log out → logs out with confirmation
  • tap Delete account → confirmation flow
  • tap Terms/Privacy → opens in browser
UX notes
  • Log out should be easy to find but not accidentally tappable. Don't hide it — that's a dark pattern.
  • Delete account must have a two-step confirmation: "Are you sure?" then "Type DELETE to confirm". Irreversible actions need friction.
  • Notification toggles should use native iOS/Android switch components — users recognise them instantly.
  • Keep settings minimal. Every extra setting is a decision a user has to make. Add only what's truly needed.

Part 3 — Mobile-first UX recommendations
1
Thumb zone first
On mobile, users hold their phone with one hand and reach with their thumb. The bottom 40% of the screen is the "easy zone." Put the most important actions there — like, save, the create button, the navigation bar. Put titles and info at the top where it's safe from accidental taps.
Example: The sticky action bar (like, save, share) sits at the bottom of the recipe detail page, reachable with one thumb while the other hand holds a wooden spoon.
2
Make every tap target at least 44×44px
Apple's own design guidelines say no tap target should be smaller than 44x44 points. This applies to every button, icon, link, and interactive element in RecipeVerse. Small tap targets frustrate users and cause mis-taps. When in doubt, make it bigger.
Example: The ingredient checkboxes on the recipe detail page should be 44px tall even if the ingredient name is short — fat finger-friendly.
3
Optimistic UI — respond instantly, confirm later
When a user taps the heart, fill it immediately. When they tap save, show the bookmark filled immediately. Don't wait for the server to respond before updating the UI. If the API call fails, revert the change. This makes the app feel native-speed even on slow connections.
Example: Tap like → heart fills orange instantly → API call runs in background → if it fails, show "Couldn't like right now. Try again." and revert the heart.
4
Never show a blank screen
Every empty state should guide the user forward. Empty feed? Show trending + "Follow creators to personalise." No saved recipes? Show "Save a recipe you love." Zero notifications? Show "Share a recipe to start getting feedback." Empty states are opportunities, not dead ends.
Example: New user with 0 follows sees: "Discover creators to follow →" with a button that goes directly to Explore. Never just an empty white screen.
5
Loading states for everything async
Whenever data is loading, show a skeleton screen (grey placeholder shapes in the layout) instead of a spinner. Skeleton screens feel faster because the user can see the shape of what's coming. Use a spinner only for actions (posting a comment, uploading a file).
Example: Feed opens → skeleton cards appear instantly → real recipe cards fade in as they load. The user never sees a blank white screen during loading.
6
Cooking mode on recipe detail
When a user is actively cooking, their hands are dirty and their phone is on the counter. Design for this: auto-keep the screen awake, use extra-large step text, make ingredient checkboxes huge, and put a "Cooking mode" button that zooms into step-by-step view full screen.
Example: Tapping "Start cooking" enters a full-screen step view. Step 1 fills the whole screen. Tap anywhere to advance to Step 2. No nav bar, no distractions.
Part 4 — Recipe creation UX best practices
The core challenge: Recipe creation is the hardest thing to make easy. A full recipe has 8+ fields. If it feels like a tax form, creators won't post. If it feels like texting a friend, they'll post every day.
🎙️ Voice creation flow
1
One tap to start
The mic button starts recording immediately. No "are you sure?" prompt. Reduce friction to zero.
2
Visual feedback
Waveform animation shows voice is being captured. Timer shows how long they've recorded.
3
Magic moment
AI processes in 5–8 seconds. Show a warm loading message, not a spinner.
4
Review all fields
All fields pre-filled. User scrolls through, fixes any errors, adds a photo.
5
One tap to publish
Big orange Publish button. Celebration animation on success ("Your recipe is live! 🎉").
✍️ Typed creation flow
1
Title first
Auto-focus on the title field. First thing typed sets the tone. Keep this field large and inviting.
2
Ingredients as a list
Each ingredient is one line. Tap "+" to add another. Never a big text box — lists feel manageable.
3
Steps as numbered blocks
Same pattern as ingredients. One step = one input. Drag handle to reorder. Tap "+" to add.
4
Quick fields
Cook time, servings, difficulty — use pickers and steppers, not free-text inputs. Faster to fill.
5
Photo last
Photo upload is at the end — many users won't have a photo ready. Make it optional but encouraged.
✓ Do this
  • Auto-save drafts every 30 seconds silently
  • Show a progress indicator ("3 of 5 sections complete")
  • Use pickers for cook time (5, 10, 15 min increments)
  • Let users reorder ingredients and steps by dragging
  • Show a preview of how the recipe will look before publishing
  • Celebrate when they publish with a brief animation
  • Make the keyboard dismiss when tapping outside a field
  • Add a character counter on description (max 500)
  • Pre-fill cuisine based on recently used
✗ Don't do this
  • Don't make any field mandatory except title + 1 ingredient
  • Don't show all fields at once — progressive disclosure
  • Don't use a single giant text area for all ingredients
  • Don't lose draft data if the app crashes or they navigate away
  • Don't require a photo to publish — many creators will skip it
  • Don't use dropdowns for cuisine — use horizontal scrollable chips
  • Don't show a wall of tags to select from — show top 10, + search
  • Don't auto-publish — always require an explicit Publish tap
