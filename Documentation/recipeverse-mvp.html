1
The MVP mindset
What is an MVP?
An MVP (Minimum Viable Product) is the smallest version of your product that still solves the core problem. It is not a half-built app. It is a fully working, polished product — just focused on the one or two things that matter most. Everything else waits.

Think of it like opening a restaurant. Your MVP is not the full menu, the private dining room, and the dessert bar. It is the one dish you make better than anyone else, served from a clean table. Once people love that dish, you add more.
The core question to ask every feature
"If we remove this feature, does the app still work and deliver value?" If yes, cut it. If no, keep it. This is the only filter you need for MVP decisions.
RecipeVerse's MVP core promise
A food lover can share a recipe in under 2 minutes — by voice, by typing, or by pasting a link — and instantly reach a community of people who will cook it, save it, and follow them.
The golden rule: Ship in 8 weeks. A launched product with 10 features beats an unfinished product with 30. Real user feedback is worth 100x more than internal opinions.
2
Features that must be in v1
The must-haves

Every feature below earns its place in the MVP by directly enabling the core promise. Each one has a clear reason it cannot be cut.

🔐
User sign up & profile
Why it must be in v1: Without accounts, there is no community. A profile is a creator's identity — their recipe portfolio, follower count, and bio. Every social action (like, follow, comment) requires a logged-in user. This is the foundation everything else is built on.
Example: Amara signs up with Google in 8 seconds, picks her username @amaracooks, adds a bio, and starts posting immediately.
📝
Recipe creation (typed)
Why it must be in v1: This is the core product. Without recipe creation, there is nothing to share, discover, or save. The typed form is the baseline — reliable, works for everyone, and gives us something to improve upon with AI and voice.
Example: Marco takes 5 minutes to type out his carbonara recipe — title, 6 ingredients with quantities, 4 steps, 20-min cook time, and a cover photo.
🎙️
Voice-to-recipe (AI)
Why it must be in v1: This is RecipeVerse's signature differentiator. No other platform has it. It removes the single biggest barrier to recipe creation — typing. If voice works well at launch, it becomes a word-of-mouth growth driver. "I just spoke my recipe and the app wrote it for me" is the kind of thing people tell their friends.
Example: David speaks for 90 seconds about his BBQ brisket. The app produces a fully structured recipe with 8 ingredients, 5 steps, and a 6-hour cook time. He fixes one spelling, then publishes.
🔗
One-click social import
Why it must be in v1: Food creators already have recipes on Instagram and YouTube. Asking them to re-type everything is the fastest way to lose them. One-click import removes all friction. A creator can move their entire content library to RecipeVerse in an afternoon — which means more content at launch, faster.
Example: Marco pastes his YouTube pasta tutorial URL. In 12 seconds, RecipeVerse fills in the title, 7 ingredients, 6 steps, and attaches the video. He hits publish.
📸
Photo & video upload
Why it must be in v1: Food is visual. A recipe with a beautiful photo gets 3x more engagement than text alone. Without media upload, RecipeVerse looks like a plain text blog — not a social platform. Cover images and step photos are the minimum. Short video is a strong addition.
Example: Amara uploads a photo of her jollof rice as the cover. She also adds a photo for step 3 showing the right colour the tomato sauce should turn.
🏠
Home feed
Why it must be in v1: The feed is why users come back. After signing up, if users see a blank screen, they leave. The feed surfaces content from followed creators — it is the habit loop of the app. Without it, RecipeVerse is just a recipe database, not a social platform.
Example: Ji-Yeon opens the app Monday morning and sees 4 new recipes from creators she follows. She saves 2, likes 1, and comments on another.
🔭
Explore page
Why it must be in v1: New users have no follows yet, so their feed is empty. The explore page is the answer — it shows trending and new recipes to everyone. It is also how recipes go viral and how new creators get discovered. Without it, the platform feels dead to first-time visitors.
Example: A new user with zero follows lands on Explore and immediately sees trending recipes. Within 3 minutes they've followed 5 creators and saved 2 recipes.
👥
Follow system
Why it must be in v1: Following is what transforms RecipeVerse from a recipe website into a social platform. It creates the relationship between creator and audience. It also powers the home feed — no follows, no personalized feed. Without this, there is no community, just content.
Example: Ji-Yeon visits Marco's profile, sees his 12 pasta recipes, and taps Follow. From now on, every recipe Marco publishes appears in her feed automatically.
❤️
Likes & saves
Why it must be in v1: Likes give creators instant feedback — they know their content is appreciated. Saves give users a personal recipe box — their collection of recipes to cook. Both are the simplest engagement actions and the ones users do most frequently. They're also essential social signals that surface good content.
Example: David likes 8 recipes on his first session. He saves 3 into a new collection he creates called "Backyard BBQ weekend."
💬
Comments & star ratings
Why it must be in v1: Comments are the conversation layer of RecipeVerse. "I made this and added extra garlic — amazing!" is the kind of engagement that builds community. Ratings help users trust recipes before cooking them. Both drive creators to post more because they feel heard and appreciated.
Example: Ji-Yeon makes Marco's carbonara, rates it 5 stars, and comments "Tried this last night — my family loved it. I added some black pepper at the end." Marco replies with a thank you.
🔍
Search & filters
Why it must be in v1: People arrive at RecipeVerse with intent — "I want a quick vegetarian pasta." Without search, they can't find it. Good search is also how the platform scales: as thousands of recipes are added, search is the only way to navigate them. It's also how new creators get discovered by people who weren't already following them.
Example: David searches "brisket" and filters by "Under 2 hours" and "American BBQ." He gets 7 relevant recipes ranked by rating.
🔔
In-app notifications
Why it must be in v1: Notifications are the mechanism that brings users back. Without them, a creator publishes a recipe and never knows anyone liked it. A user gets followed and never knows. Notifications close the feedback loop — they make the platform feel alive and responsive. Without this, retention collapses.
Example: Amara gets a notification — "Ji-Yeon saved your Jollof Rice recipe." She opens the app, sees 3 more likes, and feels motivated to post again tomorrow.
📋
Recipe detail page
Why it must be in v1: This is where users actually read and cook from a recipe. It needs to be clean, easy to follow while cooking (large text, step-by-step layout), and SEO-optimized so Google sends organic traffic. A well-designed recipe page is also a shareable link — every share is free marketing.
Example: Ji-Yeon shares Marco's carbonara page to her WhatsApp group. Her friend clicks the link, reads the recipe on RecipeVerse, signs up, and follows Marco.
🚩
Content reporting / flagging
Why it must be in v1: Every platform needs safety tools from day one. Without them, one piece of spam or inappropriate content can damage trust with all users. Flagging lets the community police itself and gives you a queue to review. It's cheap to build and expensive not to have.
Example: A user spots a spam recipe with a suspicious external link. They tap Report → Spam. It goes into a review queue. An admin removes it within 24 hours.
3
Features to postpone
Cut for now
Cutting is not failing. Every feature below will get built — just not in the first 8 weeks. Postponing them keeps the team focused, the codebase clean, and the launch on time.

Phase 2 — After launch (Weeks 9–14)

📱
Mobile app (iOS & Android)
Web works on mobile browsers. Build the native app after you've validated the product with real users. Don't invest 4 weeks in native before you know what works.
💰
Tips & creator monetization
Monetization needs a user base first. No one tips a creator with 10 followers. Build this after you have active, engaged creators who are asking for it.
🎬
Video reels / shorts
Short video is a great feature but expensive to build and host. Photo + recipe card gets you 80% of the value for 20% of the effort.
📊
Creator analytics dashboard
Creators need an audience first. Analytics become valuable once a creator has 100+ followers and wants to understand their growth.
📚
Digital cookbooks / collections selling
Requires payment infrastructure and significant UX work. Valuable in Phase 2 once creators have proven content.
🔔
Push notifications
Requires a native app. In-app notifications are sufficient for web MVP and keep users who are already in the app engaged.

Phase 3 — Scale (Weeks 15–20)

🍽️
Meal planner & shopping list
Powerful retention feature — but only once users have a substantial saved recipe collection. Build after users have 10+ saves on average.
📡
Live cooking sessions
Requires real-time infrastructure (WebRTC / streaming) which is complex and expensive. Needs a large active user base to make live shows viable.
🤝
Brand marketplace
Needs 50k+ active users to attract brand interest. This is a Phase 3 revenue channel once the platform has proven reach.
🤖
AI ingredient suggestions
"What can I cook with chicken and rice?" is a great feature but not core to the creator-first MVP. Add once the recipe library is large enough to give good suggestions.
🌍
Multilingual support
English-first is the right MVP call. Localisation is expensive to maintain correctly. Add top-demand languages (Spanish, French, Arabic) after launch based on where users come from.
💳
Paid subscriptions (RecipeVerse Pro)
Introducing a paywall before establishing strong free value kills growth. Free + engaged is worth far more than paid + small at this stage.

What success looks like in 90 days
For: Founders

Success at 90 days is not about millions of users. It is about proving the core assumptions of the business — that people want to share recipes here, that they come back, and that the AI features genuinely help.

At this point, begin Phase 2 — the mobile app, creator tips, and the features your most engaged users are asking for.

The one metric that matters most

Week-4 retention rate
Of everyone who signs up, what percentage is still using RecipeVerse 4 weeks later? This single number tells you whether RecipeVerse has become a habit. A 4-week retention above 25% means you have something real. Below 15% means the core experience needs to improve before investing in growth. Everything else is noise until this number is healthy.
The 90-day reality check: If at day 90 you have 50 creators posting regularly, 5,000 active users saving and commenting on recipes, and voice upload being used by 30%+ of creators — you have proven the concept. That is a fundable, buildable business. The numbers above are targets, not guarantees. What matters most is the quality of the engagement, not just the quantity.
