Product vision
For: Everyone
Vision statement
"To become the world's home for food creators — a place where anyone, from a home cook in Lagos to a chef in Tokyo, can share their culture through food, build a real audience, and earn a living from their passion."
Mission
Make it as easy as possible for anyone to share a recipe — whether they type it, record it with their voice, or import it from social media — and connect them with a community that loves food as much as they do.

RecipeVerse is the first place food creators post. Millions of recipes from every culture live on the platform. Thousands of creators earn meaningful income. It is the go-to destination to discover, save, and cook new recipes.

Problem statement
For: Everyone
🍳 For home cooks & creators
There is no dedicated social platform built for food. Creators are forced to split their presence across Instagram (photos), YouTube (videos), and personal blogs (text). They spend more time managing platforms than creating content — and they earn little to nothing for their work.
🔍 For recipe seekers
Finding a reliable, well-structured recipe from a trusted creator is hard. Search results are cluttered with SEO-stuffed blogs. Recipes on Instagram are buried in captions. There's no single place to discover, save, and organize recipes you actually want to cook.
📝 The creation barrier
Creating a well-structured recipe post is time-consuming. You need a title, ingredient list with exact quantities, numbered steps, cook time, servings, tags, and photos. Most home cooks give up before finishing. RecipeVerse solves this with voice recording and AI — speak your recipe and the platform formats it for you automatically.
The core insight: Food is the most universal human language. Every culture has recipes. Yet there is no platform built specifically for the food creator community. RecipeVerse is that platform.

Functional requirements
For: Developers · Designers

These describe exactly what the system must be able to do. Each requirement has an ID, description, and acceptance criteria (the checklist a developer uses to confirm it's done correctly).

FR-01 · User authentication
Users must be able to create an account and log in securely
Support sign-up and login via Google OAuth and email/password. Users must verify their email before posting. Passwords must be hashed and never stored in plain text. Session tokens must expire after 30 days of inactivity.
Acceptance criteria
  • User can sign up with Google in under 10 seconds
  • User can sign up with email and receives a verification email
  • Unverified users can browse but cannot post
  • Incorrect password shows a clear error message
  • User can reset password via email
FR-02 · Recipe creation (typed)
Users must be able to create a structured recipe manually
A recipe must include: title (required), description, at least one ingredient with name and quantity, at least one step, and a cuisine tag. Cover image is optional at creation but encouraged. Recipes can be saved as drafts or published immediately.
Acceptance criteria
  • Form validates required fields before allowing publish
  • Ingredients can be added, reordered, and deleted
  • Steps can be added, reordered, and deleted
  • Draft saves automatically every 30 seconds
  • Published recipe appears in creator's profile and feed immediately
FR-03 · Voice-to-recipe AI
Users must be able to record a recipe by speaking and have it auto-structured
User taps the microphone button and speaks their recipe naturally. Audio is sent to OpenAI Whisper for transcription. The transcript is then sent to the AI (Claude/GPT-4o) with a structured prompt to extract: title, ingredients (with quantity and unit), steps (numbered), cook time, servings, difficulty, and notes. All fields are displayed in an editable review screen before publishing.
Acceptance criteria
  • Recording starts and stops with a single tap
  • Processing and field population completes in under 10 seconds
  • All extracted fields are editable before publishing
  • If AI confidence is low, user is shown a warning to review carefully
  • Works on web (MediaRecorder API) and mobile (expo-av)
  • Minimum recording length: 15 seconds. Maximum: 10 minutes.
FR-04 · One-click social import
Users must be able to import a recipe from Instagram, YouTube, or Facebook by pasting a URL
User pastes a public URL from Instagram, YouTube, or Facebook. The system fetches the page content (caption, description, video transcript if available) using a scraping/API service. The AI then extracts recipe data and populates all fields. User reviews before publishing. A "Originally posted on [platform]" credit link is auto-added.
Acceptance criteria
  • Supports URLs from Instagram, YouTube, and Facebook
  • Shows a loading state while fetching and processing
  • All extracted fields are editable before publishing
  • If URL is private or unreachable, shows a clear error message
  • Original source credit link is always included
  • User must confirm they have rights to the content
FR-05 · Feed & discovery
Users must see a personalized home feed and a general explore page
Home feed shows the latest recipes from followed creators, sorted by recency. New users with no follows see trending recipes. Explore page shows all public recipes filterable by cuisine, dietary preference, cook time, difficulty, and sort order (trending, newest, top rated).
Acceptance criteria
  • Feed loads within 2 seconds on a standard connection
  • Infinite scroll loads 20 recipes at a time
  • New users see trending recipes, not an empty feed
  • Explore filters can be combined (e.g. Italian + Vegetarian + Under 30 min)
  • Recipes from blocked users are never shown
FR-06 · Search
Users must be able to search for recipes and creators
Full-text search across recipe titles, ingredient names, tags, and creator usernames. Search results are ranked by relevance and can be filtered.
Acceptance criteria
  • Search results appear in under 1 second
  • Searching "chicken" returns recipes containing chicken as an ingredient
  • Searching "@marco" returns creator profiles
  • Empty results show helpful suggestions, not a blank page
FR-07 · Social interactions
Users must be able to like, save, comment, rate, and follow
Like: toggle like on any recipe. Save: add recipe to a named collection. Comment: post a text comment with optional photo. Rate: leave 1–5 stars with optional written review. Follow: follow/unfollow any public user. All actions are reflected in real time.
Acceptance criteria
  • Like count updates instantly without page refresh
  • User can create multiple named collections
  • Comments support threaded replies up to 2 levels deep
  • Only one rating per user per recipe (can be updated)
  • Follow action triggers a notification to the followed user
FR-08 · Notifications
Users must receive in-app notifications for key activity
Trigger notifications for: new follower, like on your recipe, comment on your recipe, reply to your comment, and new recipe from someone you follow.
Acceptance criteria
  • Notification bell shows unread count badge
  • Clicking a notification navigates to the relevant content
  • User can mark all as read
  • User can turn off individual notification types in settings

Risks & assumptions
For: Founders · Developers

Key risks

High
Empty platform at launch ("cold start" problem)
Mitigation: Recruit 50–100 food creators before launch day. Pre-seed with 500+ quality recipes. Partner with food bloggers for early content. Launch with a "Founding Creator" badge program.
High
Voice AI inaccuracy with accents, dialects, or unusual ingredients
Mitigation: Always show a full editable review screen before publish. Highlight low-confidence fields in yellow. Continuously improve the AI prompt based on real user corrections.
Medium
Instagram/Facebook API restrictions block social import
Mitigation: Use third-party scraping services (Apify, RapidAPI) as primary method. Build fallback: user can paste caption text manually if URL fails. Monitor platform policy changes.
Medium
Creators leave if they don't see audience growth
Mitigation: Show creators their analytics from day one (views, saves, followers). Feature top creators on the explore page. Launch monetization (tips) as early as possible in Phase 2.
Medium
Competition from Instagram, TikTok adding recipe features
Mitigation: Go deep on what they can never build: structured recipe format, ingredient search, AI tools, creator monetization, recipe-first community. Become the best vertical platform, not a general social app.
Low
Copyright issues from social import feature
Mitigation: Require users to confirm ownership rights before importing. Add source credit links automatically. Build a DMCA takedown process before launch.
Low
AI costs spike unexpectedly at scale
Mitigation: At scale this is a good problem. Rate-limit free users (e.g. 5 voice uploads/month). Offer unlimited AI features as a Pro plan perk. Cache common AI responses.

Suggested launch strategy
For: Founders

A great product with a bad launch fails. A good product with a smart launch can win. Here is a practical, startup-friendly launch plan.

1
Pre-launch: Build your founding creator community (Weeks 1–6)
Before a single line of product code is written, find 50–100 food creators who will seed the platform with content. Reach out on Instagram and TikTok to creators with 1k–50k followers. Offer them a "Founding Creator" badge, early access, and a promise of being featured at launch. Their content is what early users will see first.
2
Waitlist: Build anticipation before launch (Weeks 4–8)
Create a simple landing page with a waitlist sign-up. Share it in food communities on Reddit (r/Cooking, r/FoodPorn), Facebook cooking groups, and food-focused Discord servers. Aim for 2,000+ signups before launch day. This gives you an instant audience the moment the app goes live.
3
Closed beta: Test with real users (Week 8)
Invite the first 200 people from your waitlist for a 1-week closed beta. Watch how they use the app. What do they get stuck on? Do they complete recipe uploads? Do they follow other users? Fix the biggest problems before opening to everyone.
4
Public launch: Make noise on launch day (Week 9)
Post on Product Hunt. Have your founding creators post "I just shared my first recipe on RecipeVerse" on their channels simultaneously. Post in food communities. Reach out to food journalists and bloggers. The goal: 1,000 sign-ups in the first 48 hours. One well-placed post from a creator with 50k followers can drive this alone.
5
Growth loop: Turn users into recruiters (Ongoing)
Build a share mechanism into every recipe. Every published recipe has a shareable link with a RecipeVerse watermark. When people share recipes on WhatsApp, Instagram Stories, or Twitter, they market RecipeVerse for free. Every view of a shared recipe is a potential new user. This is the viral loop that drives organic growth.
6
Retention: Keep users coming back (Months 2–3)
The biggest risk after launch is that users sign up and never return. Focus relentlessly on week-2 retention. Send a weekly email digest: "5 new recipes from creators you follow." Feature a "Recipe of the Week" on the explore page. Give creators tools to grow: analytics, featured spots, and eventually the ability to earn — because creators who are growing never leave.
One thing to remember: Your first 100 users are the most important. Talk to every single one of them. Ask what they love, what's broken, and what's missing. The best product improvements in your first 3 months will come from conversations, not analytics.
